    A Short Profile and Philosophy of Shri SBPBK Satyanarayana Rao

    -- Justice P. Kodanda Ramayya

    Sri SBPBK was born in a Zamindar family of Kapileswarapuram in East Godavari District on 23.09.1921. His full name is Sreebalusu Prabhakara Buchi Krishna Satyanarayana Rao. He was graduated from the Presidency College, Madras in 1944 and has a record of 60 years of public life covering all fields of social and cultural activities and occupying higher posts. He was the chairman of Zilla Parishad for 12 years and Member of A.P. Legislative Council representing North Circar Districts Graduates Constituency for two terms in 1958 and 1980. He was Member of Andhra University Syndicate from 1981 to 1984. He was a Member of A.P. Sangeetha Nataka Academy from 1981 to 84. He was Chairman of Srisailam Devasthanam Trust Board from 1977 to 1980. A pioneer of industrial development in sugar manufacture and textiles along with soft drinks and IMFL since 1955 upto date. Founder President of S.R.V.B.S.J.B. Maharani College, Peddapuram since 1967. He established Veda Pathasala and Hari Katha Pathasala at Kapileswarapuram since 1973 upto date. He got Vijaya Ratna by the International Friendship Society of India, New Delhi. He was Elected Member of Lok Sabha from Rajahmundry constituency. Served as Minister for State for Agriculture for one year and there after continued as Member of Parliament for the rest of the term i.e., 13.10.1999 to 7.02.2004.

    During the term he was the Chairman of Zilla Parishad, he made several developments in the education of girls and tribal and backward areas and extra curricular activities like songs and dramas. It was considered to be a golden era. He took initiative for starting Kritiventi Perraju Panthulu Junior Collge at Ramachandrapuram and also Agricultural Polytechnic at Ramachandrapuram in his name. He got several moral instruction and also teaching of spiritual lessons printed and supplied to all secondary schools in the district.

    During Gandhi Centenary Celebrations, he organized Essay Writing Competition for the students of High Schools in the District and for those students who have come out successful in the competitions, he also organized visit of all important places like Sabarmathi Ashram, Wardha, Birla Mandir, etc. places connected with Gandhiji’s life and also made available the meeting of Smt. Indira Gandhi who had appreciated their visit and blessed them.

    Tracing the beginning of irrigation systems in Andhra Pradesh, he got details of the life of Sri ARTHUR COTTON who built the Godavari and Krishna Ayacuts nearly 130 years ago fully investigated and got his biography published to commemorate his memory. This book is considered to be a standard book of its kind by the Irrigation Department. Sri Satyanarayana Rao was affectionately called as “UPADHYAYA BANDHU”. Another activity of irrigation is when there is a breach to the Godavari Bund in the year 1953 and 1956 at Kapileswarapuram and Kulla, the services rendered to flood victims by Sri Rao were appreciated by Sri Chandulal Trivedi, the then Governor of Andhra Pradesh.

    During the Pakistan war, East Godavari District secured No.1 position in collecting donations to war fund and gold under his able leadership and the then Prime Minister, Lal Bahadur Sastry made a special visit to the District and personally received the gold collected from Sri Satyanarayana Rao.

    During his capacity as M.P., he utilized Constituency Development Fund and other Development Funds to a tune of 12.17 crores for Rural Development taking about 5 to 600 works towards creating infrastructure like roads and bridges and helping the poor SCs, STs and BCs and other Minorities.

    ‘‘In Industries, he got employed nearly 15000 people directly or indirectly. For such innovative work, he was honoured with the title of ‘VIJAYA RATNA’.’’

    On the cultural activities, besides starting Harikatha Patasala and Veda Patasala at Kapileswarapuram, he distributed 18 Lalita Kala Puraskarams and 18 Sahitya Puraskarams giving outstanding persons in those fields respectively. He loved all those persons as members of the family and they in their turn considered that they are the members of his family. That is how he loved people and people loved him.

    He was born in a family of Zamindars, a noble family and his actions and utterances are nobler and ideal for other contemporaries to follow. We can call him ‘Raja Satyanarayana’.

    He has got a great regard for his father Sri B.C. Sarvaraya, a majestic personality with his turban and hence, he established a number of institutions in his name, both industrial, financial and educational.

    His brother Sri B.P. Pattabhirama Rao occupied the post of Minister of State for Industries for some time and also for Finance for some time during the period when Smt. Indira Gandhi was the Prime Minister.

    Subhashitha Ratna Mala

    Sri S.B.P.B.K. published Subhashitha Ratna Mala in nine volumes published by Sarvaraya Sugars covering different religions

    • Bhagavat Gita
    • Utterances of Sankara Bhagavat Pada
    • Utterances of Goutama Budha
    • Valmiki
    • Christ
    • Maha Bharata by Veda Vyasa
    • Prophet Mohammed
    • Sri Ramanuja
    • Sri Nanak

    Thus, he has established the idea of integration of all religions together, in this multi-religious country of ours. In his units of industry and even in cultural units, he employed all people of Christians, Muslims, Dalits and Non-Dalits as he has imbibed the norm of this Vedic religion, which is not a religion but a way of life. Thus, he helped for the integration of the nation very much in his activity and also in his utterances

    Sri S.B.P.B.K. has a deep knowledge of Sruti, Smruthi, Purana and Itihasas. He published 20 to 25 books of authors of great erudite scholarship of ancient times and himself read them intensively and understood the philosophy behind those utterances. Accordingly, he got abiding faith in the utterance of Sage Veda Vyasa, the author of Maha Bharata, who said, “I will disclose a great secret – there is no position superior to that of humanity”, and hence, every intelligent man should try to acquire liberation of the soul, which is known as Atma Siddhi by Veda, as man alone is endowed with the faculty of acquiring liberation of soul. For that, one should follow the concept of Dharma, a duty oriented, which involves sacrifice and said that man should follow five concepts of ‘Dharmam, Srutam, Tapas, Diksha, Vyavasayam’ i.e., earn money on proper way, study subject, concentrate on work, take a owe having an object of establishing institutions of permanent value and for that purpose constantly make effort which is last thing called as Vyavasaya. He did all these things with such a dedicated mind that he created a source for Dharmic work.

    Unlike Pandit Madan Mohan Malaviya who declared that ‘I am the greatest beggar’, and secured donations from several persons, Sri S.B.P.B.K. secured a source for Dharmik work himself, and used the same for Dharmik work. For this work of Pravruthi Marga, he established number of units -industrial and financial and the social and cultural work established by him constantly grew from strength to strength in view of the permanent source of wealth from those units. Hence he believes strongly that surplus money is not his and it is intended for distribution and he did accordingly.

    Finally, we can sum up his philosophy in life. One conspicuous feature of his life is, he has no children. Himself or his Dharmapatni Smt. Rajarajeswaramma do not have any such feeling of the absence of progeny at any time. They both accept with full faith the utterance of the Sruti that every human being should attain immortality by practicing Pravruthi Dharma initially, by working vigorously to secure wealth and in the end of life he should attain immortality by sacrificing/discarding all activities, progeny and wealth. Not by work, not by progeny, not by wealth, by renunciation alone one attains immortality – tayagenaikenamrtatva manasuh. Sri S.B.P.B.K. followed these concepts and ended his life with total sacrifice.

    He lived 90 years and his 60 years career clearly warrants a posthumous national award to be given to his personality by the Union of India.

    Srimad Ramayana & Mahabharatam,
    Our National Epics Promote Integration Of Our Nation

    " Narayanam Namaskrutya Naramchaiva Narothamam
    Devim Saraswatheem Vyasam Tato Jaya Mudeerayeth

    Veda Vyasa Gunavasa Vidyadisa Satamvasa
    Mam Nirasam Gataklesam Kurvanasam Harenisam

    Chandrasekharendra Saraswathi Guru Devo Nama: "

    We have two Itihasas – One Srimad Ramayana and another Sri Mahabharata.

    These two are heroic histories of our country. But they have definite message for the humanity. Ramayana is verily the Veda transmuted into Kavya by Sage Valmiki. Sri Mahabharata is known as 5th Veda containing the essence of Vedas and experience of sages who practiced those concepts. Hence both of them embody the concepts mentioned in Veda. Hence, we have to correlate the vedic hymns with the concepts given in these two epics by sages. The message of both these epics is the same, namely, man’s duty to perform Dharma in life. Ramayana says, ‘Dharmasaram Idam Jagat’ from Dharma follows wealth, form Dharma comes happiness. By recourse to Dharma, one gets everything. Na sukhath Labyate sukham. Dharma does not follow from merriment.

    The epic Maha Bharata is the biggest in its composition, seven times as great as the Iliad and Odyssey combined, animating the hearts of Indians over thousands of years, which will continue to do so in future.

    Sage Veda Vyasa, herein after called, Maharsi, himself given the message of this epic at the end of this magnificent work. It is named as Bharata Savitri.

    He Said,
    • Thousands of mothers, and fathers, and hundreds of sons and wives arise in the world and depart from it. Others will arise and similarly go away. There are thousands of occasions for joy and hundreds of occasions for fears. These affect only him who is ignorant but never him that is wise”
    • With uplifted arms I am crying aloud, but nobody hears me. From Dharma originate profit and pleasure. Why should not Dharma, therefore, be sought? For the sake of pleasure or fear, or cupidity, or even for life one should not forsake Dharma
    • Dharma is eternal. Pleasure and pain are not eternal. Jiva is eternal. The cause, however, of Jivas being invested with a body is not eternal.”

    If we know the explicit and implicit meaning of this message, we will be knowing fully the scheme of his work.

    Maharsi Veda Vyasa said, “I will disclose a great secret – there is no position superior to that of humanity”. Hence, he said, every intelligent man should try to acquire liberation of the soul, which is known as Atma Siddhi by Veda, as man alone is endowed with the faculty of acquiring liberation of soul. Veda is the source of all Dharma. It prescribes the means for Atma Siddhi in two ways known as Pravrtti and Nivrtti Dharma and prescribes the mode of practicing the same.

    The concept of Dharma is a duty oriented. It involves sacrifice. The summery thereof:-
    • Pravrthi Dhrama involves sacrificing in some measure’ Nivrthi Dharma total sacrifice’. One in a million alone can follow it. Pravrthi Dharma must be followed by all.
    • Maharshi examined all Dhramas mentioned in Sruti, Smrti, Puranas and also Acharas (traditional concepts of Dharma).
    • Maharshi said ‘one should practice 3 essential dharmas- Dama, Daya, Dana. Keeping oneself within the bounds is Dama. Self control is its essence. Daya is compassion. Dana is charity.
    • If you fail to practice these three, you will meet with opposites – kama, krodha, lobha.- they are door ways of hell.
    • How to practice these dharmas?;- Yagna, Dana, Tapas.
      • 1. Yagna means total sacrifice,
      • 2. Dana is charities lesser degree,
      • 3. Tapas is
        • Meditation,
        • Concentration of work,
        • Doing one’s own duty

        • The first two for the society and the 3rd is for the individual.

    • Maharshi said the practice of dharma must be intensive, and hence one should without transgressing Dharma acquire srutham–science; tapas – concentration; diksa – vow; and vyavasayam - effort. One should become a first class citizen in any field he works. Maharsi said, affluence, prosperity, modesty, contentment and fame reside in skill and labour, not in idleness (MB-12-27-32).
    • You must acquire knowledge through physical science, moral science and spiritual science. Through knowledge of physical science – a study of external world, you will get money and gratify your senses and you also get awareness of several things in the world. Through moral science – a study of internal world, it gives virtue like dama, which is the basic concept for all Dharmas. Through spiritual science man gets integrated and knows how to lead an ideal manhood.
    • Nasti Siddhih Akarmanah – no one gets success unless works intensively.
    • A very significant lesson we must know in this context. That is Virat created dharma after creating beings for their well being. A direct text from Brhadranyaka Upanisad is as, follows (I, iv. 14) Yet he did not flourish. He specially projected that excellent form, dharma (righteousness). This dharma is the controller of the Ksatriaya. Therefore there is nothing higher than that. So even a weak man hopes to defeat a stronger man through dharma as one contending with the king. That dharma is verily truth, Therefore they say about a person speaking truth, ‘He speaks of dharma’ or about a person speaking of dharma, ‘He speaks of truth,’ for both these are but dharma.

        We note here two things:

      • Dharma is akin to rule of law which binds all persons including the ruler.
      • Dharma and truth are the same, so they are inter changeable words. Hence Yatho darmah thatho jayah- wherever there is darma, there lies success. Similarly Satyameva jayathe- truth also triumphs but not falsehood.
    • Dharmo Viswasya Jagatahprathista - dharma supports the universe. It is supreme means of the liberation. A significant thing is all beings protect their children. That is how the activity in the world is carried on. See the hen, a pigeon, a cow – it licks and licks the calf and make her clean. There is no reciprocity on the part of their offsprings towards their parents. A stud bull cannot recognize their mother cow. In villages they prevent it. But, there is no prohibition for it. The obligation of reciprocity is enjoined only on human beings. Dharma, the righteousness is the origin of mankind. Dharma is the nector of the gods. After leaving this body, man enjoys, through Dharma, the eternal felicity (MB-12-193-33). The gods without any exception have to take birth as mortals on earth if they violate dharma. Likewise, all mortals succeed in acquiring the dignity of gods by practicing dharma (MB-12-258-41).
    • We got political freedom and with that, we also should get social responsibility. In democracy, a citizen passes the law as a sovereign, as a subject he should obey it. On the other hand, after independence, we see increase of indiscipline, law breaking, crime, sex and drug, explosions are increasing. Hence, Swami Ranganathananda emphasized that there should be spiritual orientation of our democracy. Freedom, without citizenship qualities, is dangerous and harmful to the humanity and to the globe as a whole.

    Infirmities of democracy:

    • Plato in his work ‘Republic’ described four types of unjust societies: timocracy, oligarchy or plutocracy, democracy and tyranny or despotism, in ascending order from least unjust to the most unjust. With reference to democracy, he described democracy as the third worst state. He said: It will result into a debtor nation, people borrow at high interest from the oligarchy, they will suffer bankruptcy. All people receive equal rights. They lack discipline. Politicians with no training will lead the public. They go on catering to the whims of the people. Like oligarchy, it lacks unity because people work for selfish motives instead of for the good of the state. However, we see, in the absence of other alternative, we have to resort to democracy. However, if the cardinal principles of virtue mentioned by Socrates are imbibed, democracy will be the happiest reign.

    Ills we have experienced in this Age:

    • It is in democracy, Socrates, the founder of democracy, was executed. It is in democracy, Abraham Lincoln whose definition of democracy is ever lasting was killed. It is in democracy we killed Father of our Nation, Indira Gandhi, a dynamic personality, Rajiv Gandhi, a Gem, known as Mr. Clean.
    • We have embodied in our Constitution, equality, liberty and fraternity. They are aimed at protecting the citizen against any State action. People mistake them as political doctrines. On the other hand, Sri Aurobindo said, they are the eternal attributes of spirit and the practical recognition of the truth. How it is, I shall show when I come to definition of truth.
    • Equality is a mandate against the State, but not against co-citizen. The liberty we enjoy is really empty and unreal. Even married couple cannot go freely during night, let alone the unmarried girls. Fraternity is not even claimed to be enforceable principle. A society that aims at equality must sacrifice liberty. Similarly, a society which aims at liberty, must sacrifice equality. A man or society, so long they live in individual or communal ego, strife comes. The only remedy is the fraternity, which cannot be enforced in a court of law. However, our constitution was amended enumerating duties also indicating that the court can interpret rights in the light of those duties. Hence, we have to resort to the concept of Dharma, a practice of which by all citizens, promote cordiality among themselves.
    • Let us see what Socrates, the founder of democracy, said about the basic principles of democracy and how they should be practiced. He enunciated three basic principles for establishing an ideal state.
      • The first is dividing the society into three classes of citizens, rulers, auxiliaries, and craftsmen; making them to believe in a grand and noble life of myth of metals – gold, silver & iron.
      • The second principle is citizens should practice the four cardinal principles of virtue – wisdom, courage, discipline and justice.
      • The third principle is the ruler should be a philosopher.
    • Let us see how these two Epics embody these principles. So far the division of society is concerned, in none of the Greek cities the division is not accepted. Socrates discussed these principles with his colleagues. They objected it saying it is a most impracticable thing.
    • So far in our country, sages envisaged that the activity of the man should promote welfare of the community and the conceived the Varna and Asrama Dharmas. All varnas must sacrifice. The work of each varna is considered to be yajna.
      • Japa Yajna for the Brahmin. His strength lies in securing spiritual bliss.
      • Alamba Yajna for the ksatriya. His strength lies in protecting the people.
      • Havir Yajna for the Vaisya. His strength lies in securing richness to the country.
      • Paricharya Yajna for the last order, and he is known as karma vajrah, and his strength lies in executing several projects, including all yajnas in the country. There are varnas in Devas also. Angirasas are Brahmins. Adityas are Kshatriyas. Marutas are vysyas. Aswinis are Sudras. Those are all offices. There are no Asramas for devas.
    • The four asramas are intended for individual benefit.
      • Bhrahmacarya is for study, service, chastity, obedience and austerity.
      • Household Asrama to serve not only the humanity but also all created beings.
      • The third asrama aims that one should recede slowly from active life and make self enquiry - vana prastha – dweller of forest.
      • The fourth asrama involves total renunciation, which is optional.
    • Maharshi said this varna and asrama systems will be disregarded in subsequent ages due to jealousy as contemplated by the colleagues of Socrates. However, Sage said, let the last order rule the country, but all varnas should observe 9 minimum dharmas. They are,
      • control of anger,
      • truthfulness of speech,
      • wealth sharing,
      • forbearance,
      • begetting children upon one’s own married wife,
      • purity of conduct,
      • avoidance of quarrels,
      • simplicity,
      • maintenance of dependents,
      The ruler must follow the special dharma known as raja neeti.
    • The four Dharmas mentioned by Socrates are highlighted by sages in both these Epics.
      • Wisdom – So far as Socrates is concerned, he said, this virtue must be practiced by the ruling class. But, Maharshi said, it is a requisite for humanity - for success in this life as well as thereafter.
      • Courage – So far as this concept is concerned, Socrates said, it should be practiced by soldiers. Maharshi said, it is a requisite for all human beings, both in Pravruthi & Nivruthi Dharmas.
      • Self control – so far this third requirement is concerned, the translators of Plato’s ‘Republic’ gave different names. Some say, it is a discipline. Some say, tolerance. Cliffs Notes on Greek Classics mentioned it, as virtually self-control. Socrates said, it is a discipline in the State as a sort of ‘natural harmony’ or ‘agreement’ between the different sections of the community, where the wiser section rules, and the other sections agree to being ruled. So, to avoiding strife, he says, the virtue operates as a harmony among the community. But, if you classify it as a self-control compendiously, let us see what Maharshi said, which we call it Dama
      • Justice - So far as the fourth virtue, justice, is concerned, Socrates said, it is an important virtue and it lies at the root of all other virtues stating that nobody can be wise, courageous or disciplined unless he is also just. He summed it up as a virtue, which enables him to practice other virtues and also described it as goodness. He says that Justice is the quality that makes the existence of other virtues possible. From this, Socrates derives the principle that every man should have one job and one alone so that there may not be any overreaching. This can fairly be equated to the concept of Dharma.
    • We shall note that justice administered by state is a concept of impartiality, whereas the concept of justice as contemplated by Socrates is goodness, which is Dharma. In fact, Salmond Jurisprudence, 1948 Edition, mentions that the Justice is the good conduct, which promotes general well being of the community. This is quoted in Basu’s 6th Edition, Volume-A
    • So, the justice can be equated to Dharma. That is what our Supreme Court also recently said. Thus, the concept of Dharma is not religious concept. The word ‘Dharma’ or ‘Hindu Dharma’ denotes upholding, supporting, nourishing that which upholds, nourishes or supports the stability of the society, maintaining social order and general well-being and progress of mankind; whatever conduces to the fulfillment of these objects is Dharma, it is Hindu Dharma and ultimately ‘Sarva Dharma Samabhava’ (AIR SC ).
    • Now, we shall go to third principle of Socrates, the ruler should be philosopher.

    Why the sovereignty has come on earth:

    • Maharshi mentioned, how the sovereignty has come into force. Devas requested God of Creation to remedy the situation which is unbearable on earth. They said, “An unrestrained sexual indulgence began. Men began to say what they liked. All distinctions between clean and unclean food and between virtue and vice disappeared.” (MB-12-59-20).
    • When enquired by the Lord of Creation why such conduct come into vogue, it was replied, “Ruler began to deny the authority of Vedas, tries to show that the scriptures should be disregarded and approve all breaches of control in society, that brought out the ruin” (MB-12-79-19). F
    • Hence, it was said, this ruling of kingdom is a state craft. Rajyam sumahattantram – ruling of the kingdom involves a great statecraft and can be achieved by only an accomplished person. It can never be protected by mere candour or simplicity (MB-12-58-21&22).
    • Maharsi gave extensive lessons of 365 chapters in Santi Parva. Raja dharma alone 75 chapters, Apad dharma, and Moksha dharma, which both ruler and the ruled must follow dharma.
    • So, in Raja Dharma, the following principles are embodied:
      • Raja Kalasya Karanam- he must be responsible for the time.
      • The Ruler should possess self control. Ajitatma narapatih vijayeta katham ripun – The king should first conquer himself and try to subdue his enemies be able to conquer his enemies.
      • He should not have any attachment towards wife, money and children – Dareshana, Dhaneshana, Putreshana.
        • We have got a tradition throughout the world, that if any calamity occurred to people in respect of a particular portfolio of a Minister, he should resign. As students we are not able to follow that. Our editors simply said it is a tradition in the world. Maharsi gave this axiom of Raja Kalasya Karanam and said that we should not doubt whether the king is responsible for time or not. He says he must have such a spiritual strength to change the time as he is responsible for every untoward event that takes place in his kingdom. There is no rain, he is responsible. There is excessive rain, he is responsible. There is an earthquake or an epidemic, he is responsible. We may think that this may relate to a different age. Maharsi says, it is not. He says all meritorious actions that people perform, the king automatically gets 1/4th of the benefit, provided he is sincere in securing benefit to the people without any self interest of his own. The principle is Yadadhite, Yaddhadati, Yadjuhoti, Yadarchati – of which they recite (like scriptures) or make a gift, or making a libation (yajna) or adoring a deity (MB-12-75-7). All that benefit is entitled, and hence there is no difficulty in securing spiritual strength for protecting the people.

    COMMENTARY:

He fully explained the theory of dharma in several aspects. He defined as many as 30 dharmas. He began with the Vedic Hymn “Dharmo visvasya jagatah pratistha”[ Mahanarayana Upanisad, - 79 Anuvaka] dharma is the support of the whole universe. He amplified it saying the rules of Dharma are sanctioned for the conduct of the affairs of the world[ Lokayatrarthameveha dharmasya niyamah krtah – Santi Parva Chapter 259-4], for keeping the world together[ Lokasamgraha samyuktam – Santi Parva 259-26], for promoting advancement and growth of all beings[ Prabhavarthaya bhutanam dharma pravacanam – Santi Parva 109-10], for maintaining and upholding all beings[ Yatsyat dharanasamyuktam sa dharmahiti niscayah – Santi Parva 109-10] and for securing non-injury for all beings[ Ahimsarthaya bhutanam dharma pravacanam krtam – Santi Parva 109-11]. He also said that anything that is delightfully agreeable is Dharma[ Sarvam Priyabhyupagatam – Santi Parva 259-25] and the rules of Dharma are ancient, universal, friendly, beneficial and eternal[ Sanatanam, Sarvabhutahitam, Maitram, Puranam – Santi Parva 262-10].

    The literal meaning of dharma is Dharati Lokaniti Dharmah – Dharma bears the world; Dhryate va Janairiti Dharmah – people bear Dharma. It is a rule of conduct enjoined to every human being as a duty.

    Maharsi, having stated the several facets of Dharma, still gave all embracing working rules for humanity thus[ Anusasana parva 22-25]: Maryadayam sthito Dharmah: Keeping oneself within bounds is Dharma and Samascaivasya Laksanam – Self-control is its essence. Secondly, he summed up the theory of dharma in practice thus: “That which is antagonistic to one’s own self should never be done in respect of another. Briefly this is dharma”[ Na tat Parasya Sandadhyat Pratikulam yadatmanah – Mahabharata Udyogaparva 39-72 ].

Maharsi extensively explained these concepts in Santi Parva under the Chapters of Raja Dharma and Apadharma. Sri Rama practised all these Dharmas and showed ever lasting path to the humanity to follow his practice of these dharmas.

The sages who received him also gave him the same food. When Guha offered him delicious dishes, he refused to take them saying that he was on vow with reference to food. Similarly, he observed celibacy during the days of exile and agreed to Sita Devi accompanying him after she promised him to observe celibacy during exile. Maharsi said, the characteristics and marks of self-controlled man are forgiveness, patience, abstention from injury, impartiality, truth, sincerity, control of sense, cleverness, mildness, modesty, firmness, liberality, freedom from anger, contentment, sweetness of words, benevolence, freedom from malice. All these combined make up self-control. Finally, Maharsi said that self-control is the door to immortality. Damah Dwaramhi Amrutasreha Vedmi[ S.C-299-20]. The highest among Dharmas is self-control. Self-control increases energy.

    So far the fourth virtue of justice is concerned, Plato finally summed up as a virtue, which, enables him to practice other virtues and also, described it at the end as goodness. We shall note that justice administered by state is a concept of impartiality, whereas the concept of justice to be practised by the citizens is good conduct. Every man acts according to his self interest, but his act or conduct is said to be just only if it promotes the general well being of the community. Goodness mentioned by Socrates is Dharma. Maharsi extensively explained this concept in his work naming it as Dharma. However, it is enough if we note what he finally said about Dharma in practice: That which is antagonistic to one’s own self, should never be done in respect of another. Briefly this is Dharma.

    Thus, the four principles enunciated by Socrates are extensively practised by Sri Rama as stated by Valmiki in his Epic. He said, Rama is virtue incarnate, pious and of unfailing prowess[ Aranya Kanda, Ch.37-13]. Sri Rama conquered men by his virtue; Vedic Scholars through charity; the elders through service and his enemies on the battle field with his bow[ Ayodhya Kanda, Ch.12-29]. Truthfulness, charity, asceticism, self-abnegation, goodwill, purity, straightforwardness, learning, service to elders, these are constantly present in him[ Ayodhya Kanda, Ch.12-30]. Sri Rama himself declared his abiding faith on Dharma and said to his mother Kaika, “Commanded by you, I would myself joyfully part with, in favour of Bharata, not only the kingdom of Ayodhya and my personal property, but even Sita my wedded wife, nay my beloved life itself[ Ayodhya Kanda, Ch.19-7]. He also said, “I do not desire to live in the world as a slave to material gains; know me to be devoted to immaculate righteousness like Rishis[ Ayodhya Kanda, Ch.19-20]. Consoling brother Laksmana for the calamity of exile, he said, “O Laksmana, do not give way to remorse over the loss of fortune. To me, it matters little. Whether sovergnity falls to my lot or exile, the latter being of greater consequence (in that it will be free from worries and favourable for ascetism and afford me opportunity to redeem father’s pledge given to Kaikeyi)[ Ayodhya Kanda, Ch.22-29].

    With reference to the philosopher king as envisaged by Socrates, what is stated above clearly brings out the requirement ruler being the philosopher. Both the epics Ramayana and Mahabharata mentioned the list of philosopher kings known as Rajarsis. They were kings, but they were sages. We have notice already that Sri Rama declared Viddhimam rsibhih Tulyam Kevalam Dharma masthitam[ Valmiki Ramayana 2-19-20 ] - Know me to be devoted to immaculate righteousness like a Rsi. and also the poet describing him Ramo Vigrahavan Dharmah – Rama is the personification of righteousness. Maharsi gave two axioms about a ruler. He said Ajitatma narapatih vijayata katham ripun[ S.C.69-4] “The king should first conquer himself and try to subdue his enemies. How can a king who has not been able to conquer his own self, be able to conquer his enemies”. The other axiom being Raja Kalasya Karanam[ S.C.79-69] - Virtually king makes the age. Maharsi says, “you should not doubt whether it is the king that makes the age or it is the age that makes the king. The truth is king makes the age”. Thus, for every event, king takes responsibility.

    A minister for Railways resigns for an accident that took place on a railroad, though he is innocent for that. But the rule is he must have such moral responsibility that in his administration, no untoward incident should occur and he should take responsibility for that. This is virtually the spiritual law, with all humility the administrator should follow. This is the great tradition that is embodied in this axiom.

    Socrates says that the ruling class should have no family life so that he should not be attached to the members of the family. That is exactly the same principle that these epics say without denying the family life to the king. The principle embodied in the concepts Daresana, Dhanesana, Putresana – attachment towards wife, attachment towards money and attachment towards a son respectively must be conquered by the king. We have got a catena of rulers given in the epic, known as Rajarsis, who ruled the country in such an impersonal way without any attachment to wife, money or children. Sri Rama practiced Dharma and he secured innumerable good qualities, both general virtues and also virtues required to a ruler.

    Thus we see that the Ruler should be the Philosopher and the four virtues said to be foundation for democracy are clearly found in this epic for all human beings, to follow to make their lives successful and also become ideal members of the society.

    In this connection, it is necessary to remember that the concept of Dharma is not a mere philosophical attitude in life abandoning all action even ignoring the evil. On the other hand, the epic Ramayana enjoins that for the protection of the people even that which is sinful or wrong must invariably be done by the Ruler whose duty is to protect the people 30. Maharshi Veda Vyasa evolved from this, the principle of Niskama Dharma making the Ruler to perform his duty desireless and without any enemity.

    Sri Aurobindo observed, “Modern India since Musulman advent has accepted the politics of Canakya in preference to Vyasa’s – deep preoccupation with ethical issues of speech and action is very notable”. He further said, after the disappearance of Ksatriya caste, Brahmins were only interpreting the Bhagavad Gita, and dealt only on the element of quiescence in it. But they have not echoed Sri Krishna’s emphasis on the necessity of action as the one sure road to the goal.” He also said, “Christian and Buddhism doctrine of turning the other cheek to the smiter, is dangerous as it is impracticable and mankind has never been either able or willing to carry into practice”. Sri Aurobindo readily drew the illustration for the above principle from these two epics and in particular how Sri Rama acted. He said, “Rama’s duty as a son calls him to sacrifice himself, to leave the empire of the world and become a beggar and a hermit; he does it cheerfully and unflinchingly; but when Sita is taken from him, it is his duty as a husband to rescue her from her ravisher and as a Kstriya to put Ravana to death if he persists in his wrong-doing. This duty also he pursues with the same unflinching energy as the first. He does not shrink from the path of the right because it coincides with the path of self-interest. ………… When Ravana came for the first time to the battlefield, Sri Rama crushed his vanity and joy by breaking his bow into pieces, killing his horses and the charioteer, breaking his crown into pieces and made Ravana swoon down for some time in a state of helplessness. Thereupon Sri Rama said: You have performed a great and exceedingly terrible feat, and you are fully exhausted. You go back to your house, take rest, and come back with a new chariot and weapons afresh. Then I will show my strenght”31.

    This is the best illustration of Niskama Dharma.

    A compassion shown to an enemy even during war. Ravana was one who abducted his wife and refused to give back and was waging war to retain her permanently. Would it be possible for an ordinary man to show compassion for such an enemy? This is why Laksmana described Sri Rama “Ripunamapi Vatsala” – He is benevolent even with the enemy.

    Even after the death of Ravana, we have yet another instance which speaks volumes of Rama’s virtue of practising the Dharma and the absence of enmity. When Vibisana was reluctant to perform cremation ceremony of Ravana after his death, Sri Rama disapproved such attitude and said: “hostility should cease with death.”

    Ravana is mine as well as yours. He is a mighty warrior, richly deserves due ceremonies in accordance with the prescribed code of conduct. An ordinary man would think of throwing the body of Ravana into sea and that would finish the matter. But Sri Rama was not of that type. He is Satpurusa. He is the foremost among men. Hence, the divine Sage Narada described him as ‘Sadhu. Sri Rama said to Vibhisana, “Your brother is my brother, because, with his death, the enmity ceases” and directed him to perform due ceremonies with all reverence.

    Thus, we see, the epic Ramayana is not a mere Kavya aiming at giving pleasure to the humanity. But is very instructive. That is why, Sri Aurobindo said that Homer and Shakespeare greatest poets in the world, our epics Ramayana and Maha Bharata are built on an almost cosmic vastness of plan and take all human life and human thought as well in their scope and touch on things which Greek and Elizabethan poets could not even glimpse. Hence, he said, “These epics are therefore not a mere mass of untransmutted legend and folklore, as is ignorantly objected, but a highly artistic representation of intimate significances of life, the living presentment of a strong and noble thinking, a developed ethical and aesthetic mind and a high social and political ideal, the ensouled image of a great culture. As rich in freshness of life but immeasurably more profound and evolved in thought and substance than the Greek, as advanced in maturity of culture but more vigorous and vital and young in strength than the Latin epic poetry, the Indian epic poems were fashioned to serve a greater and complete national and cultural function and that they should have been received and absorbed by both the high and the low, the cultured and the masses and remained through twenty centuries and intimate and formative part of the life of the whole nation is of itself the strongest possible evidence of the greatness and fineness of this ancient Indian culture.

    As we are considering the relevancy of the epic to the modern man, I conclude with this statement of Annie Besant referring these two epics in her work “Sri Ramachandra”, “Despite the infuence wielded by these two books, however, their moulding power on life is not so great as once it was.     Ramayana and Mahabharata are two Itihasas – heroic histories of this country. In the West, these stories are known as epics. We have also begun to classify them as epics. It is well known that an epic is a narration of the life of heroic or legendary figures and implies that it is unauthenticated and hence a myth. Only few countries have these epics. The great epics of the West are the Iliad and the Odyssey. “The Wordsworth Encyclopedia” classified the Ramayana and Mahabharata as epics of India and stated that epics are “narrative poem or cycle of poems dealing with some great deed – often the founding of a nation or the forging of national unity – and often using religious or cosmological themes.” It is significant to note that the National character of the epic is not lost, simply because it is a religious story or a theme of a particular religion. Irrespective of religion the whole nation owns it. The second characteristic is, the epic story is always related to earth and heaven, and human beings and angels mix together. Several episodes and events cannot be believed by common man hence they are termed as myths in the west. However the epic instills greater values in humanity and they are found to be useful and essential for humanity. Even an iconoclast like Bernard Shaw acknowledged the essentialities of myths. In this country we know when the Absolute descends on earth and takes a form; it will have powers and super natural qualities. We have such experiences of several incarnations. No Indian doubts the events in these epics. Further, sages Valmiki and Veda Vyasa are the contemporaries of the heroes in these stories and actually witnessed all the events. Hence these epics are the living stories in this country and therefore they are well known Itihasas and not myths.

    RAJA NITI - STATE CRAFT
    The relevance of the concept of Dharma in the administration of State By Justice P. Kodanda Ramayya, former Judge, A.P. High Court

    Maharsi Veda Vyasa said Rajyam Sumahattantram – the ruling of the Kingdom involves a great State Craft, which can be achieved only by an accomplished person. The kingdom can never be protected by a mere candour and simplicity. The ruler must be both ferocious and soft.

    Sage Valmiki in his work Ramayana (Ch.67 & 100 of Ayodhya Khanda) stressed the need of a good ruler for a country and State Craft respectively.

    Maharsi Veda Vyasa gave extensive lessons on this subject. Under the heading Rajadharma, he said that Lord Bramhadeva composed a work on the subject of Nyaya Sastra containing one lakh Chapters. The same was condensed subsequently by Lord Siva, Indra, Brhaspati and finally Sukracarya limiting it to 1000 Chapters. Mharsi stated that Niti should take into account the hearts of men, place, time, means, tactics, alliances and causes. The topics considered in that work are:

    • The religious rites laid down in three Vedas, knowledge known as Jnana Kanda and acts necessary for the support of life such as agriculture and trade and also the rules relating to punishment of offenders who violate the laws.
    • The subject relating to Dharma, Artha, Kama and also liberation.
    • Sama, Dana, Bheda, Dandopaya – conciliation, gifts, sowing seeds of discord, punishment respectively and also toleration are stated for the guidance of the king.
    • In politics, sometimes what is regarded as unrighteous is adopted as Niti. The triple aggregate of Sthana, Vrddh and Ksya – conservation, growth and destruction to be observed by the Ruler with neighbouring State are also given.
    Personal conduct of a king

    Raja Ranjayati Prajah – The righteous and the truthful king succeeds in pleasing his subjects. The king should first conquer himself and then try to subdue his enemies. Ajitatma narapatih vijayeti katham ripun – How can a king, who has not been able to conquer his own self, conquer his enemies?

    He should be like a Vernal Sun, neither too cold nor too hot. He should treat his people as a mother treats her child. The king is the heart of his people. The king should be careful in collecting taxes. A person who wants milk should not cut the udder of the cow. As the bee gathers honey from flowers gradually, the king should follow the rule of a flower vendor and not that of a charcoal maker. The king should not keep full confidence in any one, as absolute confidence destroys both morality and profit. However, total want of confidence is worse than death. Thus, not to trust any one fully has been regarded as the greatest mysteries of statecraft. He should not keep confidence in his own son. When the opportunity comes, one should make peace with the enemy. When the time comes, one should make war with even friends. King is called ‘Rajan’ if virtue predominates. He is called ‘Vrsala’ if virtue is totally absent. A king conversant with his duties should put on many forms as the peacock displays plumes of various hues. Keenness, crookedness, truth and sincerity are the qualities, which should be present in him. He should practice impartiality. He should have the qualities of goodness if he has to acquire happiness.

    That king earns eternal merit in whose kingdom there is no wickedness, dissimulation, deception and envy. The king should practice 36 virtues and invariably avoid 36 vices that may arise in practicing those virtues. We shall illustrate them. The king should seek pleasure without attachment. He should be brave without being a brag. He should be liberal. He should not make gifts to unworthy persons. He should make alliances, but not with the wicked. He should speak the merits of others, but not of his own. He should take money from his subjects, but never from those who are good. He should distribute money, but not amongst covetous persons. He should place confidence in others, but never in that which is bad. He should be mild, but never to the offender. If the king fails to restore to a subject, the wealth stolen from him by thieves, he should make good the loss from his own treasury or in case of his inability, with the wealth taken from his dependents.

    By protecting his subjects, the king acquires one-fourth part of the merit earned by his subjects. The king should keep vigilant in protecting the kingdom, collect taxes reasonably and must provide sustenance to all citizens without anyone leaving the state for their livelihood.

    Niti is moral, concerned with the goodness or badness of a human character making distinction between right and wrong. A virtuous king must be the master of duties in whom reside forgiveness and anger, giving and taking, terror and fearlessness, chastisement and reward. The king is entitled to take one-sixth of the income of the subjects.

    The service and the love of the subjects is the most impregnable fort among the six forts mentioned in the scriptures. The king should not indulge in two things: pride and unrighteousness. He should not associate himself with those who are intoxicated with pride, who are not honest, who scoff at religion, who are insensate or negligent. The king should never be hesitant to take action even against his own son, if he violates the law. King Sagara abandoned his son Asamanjasa for his misdeeds. Similarly, Sage Uddalaka, sent his son Swetaketu away.

    Steadiness, cleverness, self-control, intelligence, health, patience, bravery and attention to the requirements of time and place: these eight qualities always multiply wealth, be it small or be it much. A little fire, fed with clarified butter, may end into a conflagration. A single seed may produce a tree, which gives a thousand seeds. A king, therefore, even when he knows that his income and expenditure are vast, should not neglect the smaller items.

    Sage Vedavyasa has devoted several topics useful for the king, such as duties towards kingdom, ways of augmenting treasury, appointing dutiful officers, protecting fort, conducting war, leading the army and meritorious regions that can be acquired by valour.

    We must notice that the exposition of sage Markandeya to Yudhisthira when later asked the former the course of action to be followed by a king. He said, “Be kind to all creatures and be devoted to their good. Love all without hating anyone. Be truthful, be self-controlled, be ever engaged in protecting your subjects. Practice virtue and avoid sin and worship the Pitrs and the celestials. Whatever you have done from ignorance, expatiate it by giving away in charity. Abandon pride, always possess humility”.

    The modern young man is under a mistaken impression that the concept of Dharma is a religious concept and he says that in the set up of democracy we require equality, liberty and fraternity, the political norms. It is equally a mistake to think that the three doctrines embodied in the Constitution of our country and other countries throughout the world are political doctrines. In fact, these three concepts are God Heads of Soul and as observed by Aurobindo:

    “Freedom, equality, brotherhood are three godheads of the soul; they cannot be really achieved through the external machinery of society or by a man so long as he lives only in the individual and the communal ego. When the ego claims liberty, it arrives at competitive individualism. When it asserts equality, it arrives first at strife, then at an attempt to ignore the variations of Nature, and, as the sole way of doing that successfully, it constructs an artificial and machine-made society. A society that pursues liberty as its ideal is unable to achieve equality; a society that aims at equality will be obliged to sacrifice liberty. For the ego to speak of fraternity is for it to speak of something contrary to its nature.”

    Hence he said, the brotherhood is the real key to the triple gospel of the idea of humanity. We cannot achieve these objects embodied in the Constitution as they are really virtues – different facets of dharma. In spite of loud proclamation of these precepts, humanity has not really won them. Is it possible to practice the precept of fraternity when persons born to the same parents look with reservation, suspicion, hatred and kill each other for property? Fraternity is not even claimed to be an enforceable principle. Equality can be enforced only against state but not against a co-citizen. The liberty we enjoy is really empty and unreal. Even married couple cannot move freely in the streets, let alone unmarried girls. Unless citizens follow Dharma and become virtuous, the state alone cannot enforce these doctrines and achieve the goal.

    Hence, Socrates, the founder of Democracy said that a citizen and ruler should practice Dharma. He enunciated three basic principles of democracy for establishing ideal state. First dividing society into three classes of citizens, rulers and auxiliaries and craftsmen. The second principle is the citizens should practice four cardinal principles of virtue viz., wisdom, courage, discipline (self-control) and justice. The third principle is ruler should be philosopher.

    We shall see how these principles are extensively explained in our epics Ramayana and Maha Bharata and there are several kings who are considered as Rajarsis – philosopher kings. Sage Valmiki gave 8 characteristics of a sound and ripe intellect. They are,

    • The desire to hear,
    • Hearing,
    • Receptivity,
    • Retentive power,
    • Reason,
    • Against proposition,
    • Faculty of apprehension,
    • Final realization.

    It includes knowledge, experience, and ability to act. Dhrti is the Sanskrit word for courage. Sage Valmiki gave six facets for this. They are Sattvam, Dhrti, Sthairyam, Viryam, Pratapa and Sauryam. They bear different meanings is indisputable, as Sage gave in one sloka all these faculties possessed by the hero Sri Rama (Valmiki Ramayana, 6-15-3).

    When we come to the third principle discipline or self-control, Maharsi Veda Vyasa said, the characteristics and marks of self-controlled man are forgiveness, patience, abstention from injury, impartiality, truth, sincerity, control of sense, cleverness, mildness, modesty, firmness, liberality, freedom from anger, contentment, sweetness of words, benevolence, freedom from malice. All these combined make up self-control. Finally, Maharsi said that self-control is the door to immortality. Damah Dwaram hi Amrutasreha Vedmi. The highest among Dharmas is self-control. Self-control increase energy.

    So far the forth virtue of justice is concerned, Plato in his work “The Republic” finally summed up as a virtue, which, enables him to practice other virtues and also, described it at the end as goodness. We shall note that justice administered by state is a concept of impartiality, whereas the concept of justice to be practiced by the citizens is good conduct. Every man acts according to his self interest, but his act or conduct is said to be just only if it promotes the general well being of the community. Goodness mentioned by Socrates is Dharma. Maharsi Veda Vyasa extensively explained this concept in his work Maha Bharata naming it as Dharma and summed up by saying, “That which is antagonistic to one’s own self, should never be done in respect of another. Briefly this is Dharma.”

    Now on the concept of philosopher king, Maharsi Veda Vyasa gave two axioms about a ruler. He said Ajitatma narapatih vijayeta katham ripun “The king should first conquer himself and try to subdue his enemies. How can a king who has not been able to conquer his own self, be able to conquer his enemies”. The other axiom being Raja Kalasya Karanam – Virtually king makes the age. Maharsi says, “you should not doubt whether it is the king that makes the age or it is the age that makes the king. The truth is king makes the age”. Thus, for every event, king takes responsibility.

    A Minister for Railways resigns for an accident that took place on a railroad, though he is innocent for that. But the rule is he must have such moral responsibility that in his administration, no untoward incident should occur and he should take responsibility for that. This is virtually the spiritual law, with all humility the administrator should follow. This is the great tradition that is embodied in this axiom.

    Socrates says that the ruling class should have no family life so that he should not be attached to the members of the family. That is exactly the same principle that these epics say without denying the family life to the king. The principle embodied in the concepts Daresana, Dhanesana, Putresana – attachment towards wife, attachment towards money and attachment towards a son respectively shall be abandoned embody these great principles for a ruler. We have got a catena of rulers given in our epics, known as Rajarsis, who ruled the country in such an impersonal way without any attachment to wife, money or children.

    So far the first principle of dividing society is concerned, it was not put into practice in any of the cities in Greece, but in our country, the class system divided the Hindu Society into four Varnas and all Varnas have to sacrifice for the community. The sacrifice ordained for Brahmanas consists of recitation of Vedas observing severe austerities, for the Ksatriyas, it consists of controlling the wild animals and protecting the humanity by sacrificing his life in the battle; for Vysyas consisting of raising crops and keeping the domestic animals, and sacrifice of Sudras is by serving humanity with dedication. However, Maharsi Veda Vyasa said that this class system will not be followed in Kali Yuga and will be abandoned. However, he mentioned the common duties of all Varnas shall be followed. They are nine in number. Those nine rules are, control of anger, truthfulness of speech, justice, forgiveness, begetting children upon one’s own married wife, purity of conduct, avoidance of quarrel, simplicity and maintenance of dependents. The basic nine principles mentioned by the sage as a common rule to all as Navaite Sarvavarnika.

    Democracy will be successful only if we practice Dharma. Hindu society is based upon Dharma – duty oriented is best suited to democracy.

    Now, we see that practice of Dharma is a basic requirement in democracy both by the ruler and the citizen. Our two epics composed by Sage Valmiki and Veda Vyasa gave the requirements of Dharma to be followed both by the citizens and the State. I may conclude with the statement of Annie Besant referring our two epics – Ramayana and Maha Bharata in her work “Sri Ramachandra”, “Despite the influence wielded by these two books, however, their moulding power on life is not so great as once it was. He belongs to the ranks of Mallinatha Suri and Pandit Jagannatha.

He also points out while translating any commentary on any work, the commentary of another leading author which appears to be more appropriate and terse on any particular aspect. Further in all his works, he brings out the ancient culture of this country based upon Vedic Hymn which declares that whole world should be happy – Lokasamasta Sukhinobhavanthu. I must also say that he is always humble in his work without any semblance of pride either because of works or the honours he secured. On the other hand, he always concludes his work saying that he is dedicating the work to Lord Sri Janaki Rama and states that this he secured that work by the grace of Lord, by the kindness of his Guru and the penance of his parents and he is only an instrument for bringing out that work. That is the height of his humility.

    In his eighties he is quite healthy and invariably invited to all Literary and Vedic Conferences and continues to work in the direction of peace loving trait of a true Indian and the nation is very proud of him.

    KAVI SAMRAT SRI VISWANATHA SATYANARAYANA

    AS I KNOW HIM

    I was a student of S.R.R. & C.V.R. College, Vijayawada during 1945-46 studying Intermediate. At that time, Sri Viswanatha Satyanarayana Garu was our Telugu Lecturer. Since then, for over three decades till he left his mortal coil in 1977, I was very intimate with him. I shall refer Sri Viswanatha Satyanarayana Garu as ‘the poet’ and the Sahitya Peetham founded in his name as ‘the Institute’. In fact, I must say, my intimacy with the poet was further blossomed due to my brother Dr. M.N. Gurunath, who is a dental surgeon at Vijayawada to whom I introduced the poet and they continued as bosom friends. He used to take care of his person and wanted to reduce his tension by playing cards, chess and even taking him invariably to a new cinema. Though there was a difference of 30 years in age, he used to address the poet in singular as ‘Neevu’. He used to warn him not to be so emotional in his speeches saying that he cannot reform these people. On the other hand, the poet used to say, “Don’t bother doctor! In every public speech I used to grow stronger; that is my mission.” Unfortunately, my brother passed away three years back though he was only a two years older than myself. He would have given the best account of the poet’s personality if he was alive. The poet used to address me as Kodanda. My brother also used the same expression referring me.

    I attended almost all the lectures of the poet both at Vijayawada and also at Andhra Saraswatha Parishad, Hyderabad. I had the great privilege of discussing with him and get clarification on all points on which he delivered the lectures and his works. Almost at the end of his life, I asked him that there is a vast difference between his lectures and his work on Ramayana as he was giving the philosophy behind the epic Ramayana, but he did not state so in the epic. For that he said, “It is true such philosophy cannot be given in the work, though I have indicated here and there.” Hence, I requested him to write such book. He agreed for the same. He wrote it and sent to me when I was an advocate at Hyderabad. After reading it, I requested him that it is necessary that he should write commentary for it, for which he protested saying, it is unusual that a person should write commentary for his own work. I quoted himself saying once, that there were such instances and hence it was an absolute necessity and he should do so. With all affection and love, he said, “Kodanda, any how I have to do this and die.” I apologized to him saying, why should he use such expression of death. In fact, it so happened that my brother reported with choking voice that “Out of affection to you he wrote the commentary also on the book and he passed away.” The book named ‘Naa Ramudu’ was the last work and it was posthumously published with an Introduction by Dr. Diwakarla

    The Institute has already published a comprehensive information about the poet’s works and his achievements and different posts occupied by him with all distinction. However, my friend Sri Kondal Rao, knowing my intimacy and my narrating several stories about the poet, wanted me to write about him ‘in English’. My mind is full of several utterances of his, on the several topics. It is not possible to enumerate them as it would be inchoate and incomplete and not fit to be a narrative to be read, as such things can be delivered orally. Hence, I intend to give in this short narrative of his personality that impressed me most, which I want to place before all general readers.

    A man means his mind. We know a particular person by his mind, his intelligence, Prajna followed by his Pratibha. A man possessing Prajna becomes a scholar or a scientist. A man possessing Pratibha becomes a poet*. The former is the maturity of the intellect and the later is blossoming of the heart. This poet has got both the qualities. Prajna and Pratibha are highlighted by his Sradha and Deeksha – faith and fervor and finally they reflect his philosophy in life. Thus, we shall notice the four aspects of our poet i.e., Prajna, Pratibha, his faith and fervor and his philosophy.

    A facet of his Prajna that is uppermost in my mind is his ability to judge men and matters. He precisely described his colleagues and poets and their calibre. He used to call poet Jashuva as a ‘Madhura Bhashi’, Dr. Diwakarla as a ‘Sadhu’, Sri Devulapally Ramanuja Rao as a ‘Lion’, Sri Srisri as a ‘Yuga Purusha’. It is necessary to give the narrative of his utterances

    Once a meeting took place at Andhra Saraswatha Parishad. Sri Jashuva was speaking on the dais. In the middle, our Poet had come. Every thing was stopped and the speaker also waited for his being seated comfortably before continuing his speech. Our poet having witnessed the applause and ovation of the meeting praising Jashwa, he said “Oh! A great poet, what is there in my poetry.” - in a satirical way and clearly as a matter of joke. Sri Jashuva replied saying, “Sri Kavi Samrat is right hundred percent. His father was stotriya – full of vedic learning and wisdom. Hence, he can easily imbibe everything and there is no greatness if he writes great poetry. My father belongs to a low ebb. Hence, if I write poetry with a great struggle, it is certainly great indeed. So, I say our Kavi Samrat is right in saying so.” Thereupon, our poet stood up and said, “Jashuva, what a sweet utterance” and also said “I was defeated.” The Telugu expression used is “Nannu Chittu Chesavayya”. That was the way of accepting any criticism against him.

    Once Sri Ramanuja Rao asked our Poet to give a discourse in writing as they wanted to publish it. It was prepared and before it was actually delivered by him, he handed over it to Dr. Diwakarla to look into it. He was asked to introduce all speakers in that meeting. While introducing our Poet, Dr. Diwakarla mentioned some of the basic principles contained in the Article prepared by the Poet. Thereupon, when our Poet stood up to speak, he grew wild with Dr. Diwakarla saying, “He cheated me for the reason that what I had to speak was already spoken by him” and delivered his speech in his own way on the same subject without referring to the Article contained in the writing. At the end of the meeting, I protested with the poet saying, Dr. Diwakarla treats him not only as a Guru, but also with paternal affection. Simply because he mentioned some of the points contained in the written script, he should not abuse him in such a wild manner. He confessed his mistake and said, take me to Diwakarla. We both of us went to his house. Our Poet immediately on seeing him said, “Avadhani (Diwakarla), you know my temperment. I am sorry for my harshness.” Thereupon, Dr. Avadhani pointed out, “This Vakil does not know what happened Sir. What you wrote in the Article is not upto the mark. But, when you spoke something brushing aside the Article, the true life of the subject has come out. So, good thing had resulted by your anger towards me, though it is a mistake which I should not have done.” Thereupon, our Poet said, “You are a Sadhu indeed”.

    I asked the poet to introduce Sri Devulapalli Ramanuja Rao Garu to see that I got invitations for all meetings held at Andhra Saraswatha Parishad. But I did not get response immediately and I asked him, “Why can’t you tell him second time,” as he told me that he already informed him. Thereupon, he said, “I cannot tell second time to him, such a liberty cannot be taken with him. He is a lion. He gives respect and commands more respect.”

    About last event of Sri Srisri, when I was a student of S.R.R. & C.V.R. College, Vijayawada, we invited him. The Principal of the College wanted our Poet to preside over the meeting on that day. We were all thinking that there will be a full debate on the question of modern poetry known as Bhava Kavitwam for which Srisri was the author and there may be any amount of tussle. On the other hand, we are all surprised as the poet praised Sri Srisri calling him as a ‘Yuga Purusha’. He said, “People who want to copy you are thinking that you have only dispensed with Chandas giving them a boon, but they forget the fire within you. The fire within you made you to write such kind of poetry, but not simply dispensing the chandas. You are a path maker of this method of poetry and you will be known as ‘Yuga Purusha’.

    Continuing the same line of thinking, we have got an excellent poem in Ramayana Kalpavriksham in Avatarika about the ancient poets. He described 9 of the stalwarts of Telugu literature giving precisely their caliber and their outstanding features. A liberal translation of the stanza reads as follows:

    “Nannayya is the second Valmiki; Tikkanna is architect of Telugu orchard; Erranna is like a Lord of Creation of all paths of poetic styles; Potana is the cassette of Bliss; Srinatha is a torrential flow of river Rasa; Krishnaraya is the author of incomparable Prabhandha; Peddanna is the filtered candy juice; Ramakrishna is the Iravatham; Nachana Somana is a type by himself.”

    I asked the Poet what is the meaning of “Okadu Nachana Somana”. He answered saying that he is a type by himself, incomparable. According to him, the 9 stalwarts cannot be substituted as they are like Nava Brahmas. About our poet, we may (these 9 stalwarts may agree) call him even after leaving his mortal coil, as ‘Kavi Samrat’ for his depth, dedication and contribution to Telugu literature.

    There is yet another instance of his ability to judge even divine characters. He said in Avatarika in the same Book, Lord Brahma Deva knows the Lord of Reality only 1/4th and Goddess Parvati knows only half of Cosmic Reality. This is based upon the philosophic axiom that there are degrees of understanding of the ultimate reality as Brahma Vidh, Brahma Vidvara, Brahma Vidh Varista. This remark on God of Creation and Goddess Parvati related to well known stories. God of Creation wanted to test Lord Sri Krishna by hiding the cow boys and calves for a period of one year. The Lord took the form of all cow boys and also the calves for a period of one year. Then Lord Brahma Deva prostrated and confessed his mistake and sought apology. Similarly, Goddess Parvati Devi wanted to test Sri Rama when he was wandering and weeping over the departure of Sita Devi when Ravana abducted her. She took the form of Sita Devi and on seeking her, Sri Rama said, “Mother! Where is Maha Deva? Why you alone have come.” Thereupon, Goddess Parvati shivered and later, Maha Deva, having come to know about this, took a owe stating that she took the form of Sita Devi and I cannot touch her body. Divine will as it were Goddess Parvati went to Daksha Yagnam and left the body by self immolation.

    Now, we will see the second aspect of faith and fervor. His faith is actually, considering his profession as a Poet, his deep indebtedness to the Poet Valmiki. Sruti declares that every human being owes debts to Pitrus, Risis and Devas. Every poet normally makes salutation to the Poet Valmiki, the Adi Kavi before he begins any work. On the other hand, our Poet’s utterances clearly show how deeply he feels the indebtedness towards Sage Valmiki and the debt we owe to him. He said, “Would this humanity able to discharge for any number of generations the debt of literary wealth owed to Valmiki.”. “Is it possible for us to discharge the debt of his giving rich faculty for preparing a Kavya?” “Is there any poet comparable with Valmiki, who is the Sukavi.” In view of this faith, he took the owe that he should write Ramayana to discharge the debt.

    Having enjoyed the Kavya Rasa and realized the importance, he took it as his mission to instill faith in Telugu people to read Kavya and secure the benefit of Kavya Rasa, which is of perennial value for humanity. Those were the days of national revolution against the British. He composed Basha Vachakamulu as Telugu textbooks from First Form to VI Form embodying the culture of this country given by Telugu poets. In his public speeches, unlike the other poets who would like to give pleasure by reading his compositions, he used to quote Maha Kavi Kalidas, Bharavi, Bhava Bhooti, Krishna Karnamrutham and Maha Bharatam - Tikkanna and Nannayya and explain in great detail the Charutva, Madhurya, Soukumarya and Sarasatva style of the poets being – the charming, sweet, delicate and full of Kavya Rasa respectively in those works. He often quoted Bartru Hari who said, for a poet the empire is not necessary, meaning thereby the poet enjoys the joy of an emperor due to Kavya Rasa. Whenever he was reminded of the experience of Tikkanna with Hariharanatha, who wanted him to translate into Telugu Maha Bharata and dedicate it to him, he used to explain in detail the sloka “Kimastimalam Kim Kaustubhamva…..” where it was confirmed by Lord that he loved Tikkanna for his revelling always in Kavya Rasa in addition to his righteous conduct. To make our Telugus awake from the slumber, he used to worship Goddess Saraswathi and could assert that Goddess Saraswathi took the form of male in him. It is well known scriptural saying that Narudro Rudra Marchayeth – unless you assume the form of Rudra you cannot worship him.

    Similarly, Jagatguru Sankara also mentioned “Saaroopam Tavapujane” –worshiping you Lord I assume your form. In that context, while paying tribute to his Guru Cellapilla Venkata Sastrigaru, the poet said, that “He was fortunate in having a sisya like myself, which privilege was not there for Nannayya and Tikkanna” This statement apparently appears to be arrogant and even his Guru asked him “whether you praised yourself or praised me.” The answer was “It is left to your goodself to decide.” On a second thought, Sri Sastrigaru said, “I am proud of him. His style is different. He was an uncommon kind of a poet and could not be brushed aside.” One can never describe him arrogant if he knows his internal surrender to Lord, in particular, the Ten Satakas embodied in his work on the Madyakkaralu. A man who surrenders to Lord absolutely like ‘Dasanudasa’ can never be described arrogant and the apparent harshness is emphasis only, for arresting the attention of the public. In fact, in his missionary zeal once he said, “This country will perish. It has began to doubt the utterances of sages.” and there were several occasion when he broke down in speeches considering the pathetic condition of our country men ignoring our heritage given by sages. An arrogant man can never get tears. Seeing the apathy of the country, he composed a book known as Sahitya Surabhi containing the works of great poets giving meaning to each sloka and the poem and their in depth meanings and the background of those writings. In the introduction he said, “the Vyamoham – the inordinate affection for English should go. We should banish English from this country in order to impress the greatness of our Telugu literature. Since it was not possible, I have to resort to this method placing before the Telugu readers the composition of great poets giving three hundred stanzas with their literary contents and background of their composition. If any one memorises these with meaning, he will become a scholar in Telugu literature, and becomes a litterateur”. I conclude by quoting his utterance in one meeting “If I fail in my challenge that one cannot become a scholar in Telugu after getting by heart with meanings these three hundred poems, I will cut my ear.” This was the depth of his faith and the height of his fervour.

    Regarding the last topic of his philosophy, I refer to his work ‘Naa Ramudu’ giving some of the extracts of the book. The Book was divided into 10 parts under 10 headings viz., Ananda Mayudu, Ananda Murthy, Avatara Murthy, Bala Ramudu, Kodanda Ramudu, Ayodhya Ramudu, Dasaratha Ramudu, Janaki Ramudu, Raghu Ramudu, Atma Ramudu. The first three and the last relate to the ultimate reality and Lord Sri Rama as God incarnation on earth and 4 to 9 relates to topics why he got the names related to each of those topics. This book constitutes the commentary on Kalpa Vriksham gives the life history of the poet, philosophy behind the epic and also constitutes a separate work as Kavya. I shall give only few extracts from the Book in three parts.

    • relating to incarnation of Sri Rama,
    • poet’s life history,
    • his spiritual experiences.

    In the first part, I shall refer the first topic Ananda Mayudu. He says, Lord is source of joy that is known as Rasah Vai Sah. He said, he had secured it twice, in his life by the grace of Sri Rama, but he cannot recollect when and how. His seeing Lord in the form of Rama is not samething as the bliss he enjoyed. He said, Brahma the Lord, the bliss and Sri Rama, all the three are one. He also mentioned what is known as Kavya Rasa sometimes by getting the joy of reading a Kavya in a way is, the first step towards the Ultimate Bliss known as Brahmananda. It is beyond all the three states of deep sleep, dream and awakefullness.

    The second topic relates to Ananda Murthy. He said Devas also come on earth combining their experiences, ideas and qualities. The third topic relates to Avatara Murthy. He said, the God takes the form by Will, but men take the form by their will coupled with their past action. The fourth topic, at the end, Atma Rama, he said, the Lord is within the core of the heart in every one. Very few can realize him. One should try to realize the same. The second part relates to his life history. He said, in his village there are two temples Santana Venugopala Swamy and also Kasi Viswanatha Swamy. His father was responsible for installing the Kasi Viswanatha Swamy Temple. His father was Puranic scholar and always used to give discourses of Bharatam and Bhagavatam. But he exhorted him to prepare a work on Ramayana. The poet said, he took only one month to prepare Veyyi Padagalu but for preparing Ramayana, he took 30 years. He recorded in this book how he got the prompting by Sri Rama more than once that he should again start writing Ramayana. He also quoted two instances where the Lord Sri Rama helped him to complete two poems and in Yudha Kanda and also at the last chapter Upa Samharana Kanda.

    Now we shall refer to his experiences. He disclosed his spiritual achievements for our benefit. Lord Sri Rama along with Sita Devi and Hanuman revealed himself to him number of times. Similarly, Maha Deva in the form of Linga and with the garland also revealed to him number of times. He said, “Lord Sri Rama is in my core of heart and prompting me to complete the work of Ramayana.” He also said I only seek the benefit of divine ecstasy by composing this work of Ramayana.

    He was quite aware of the fact that there are shortcomings, limitations about his personality comparing with Ravana such as, anger, jealousy, ignorance, thinking his poetry itself is a great achievement and hence, his objective surrender at every stage to the Lord is also revealed at the end of the chapter in Ramayana. He finally bowed to Lord Sri Rama that He was responsible to get this work done giving him sovereignty on language, sharpness and unbroken thought while composing the work. He also said, his utterances in poetry are not mere imaginations, but all experiences recorded in dreams and wakeful state. He also mentioned that he adores both Siva and Rama and dedicated the work of Ramayana to Maha Deva. He also mentioned that he described Visnu, Siva and also Sakthi in his work.

    In the later period of his life, the poet was not prepared to give any discussion on any topic, except reading his Ramayana. He loved the epic deeply. He said “I do not envy Devas for their having enjoyed Amrutham – nector as I enjoy the nector of Kavya Rasa of the pious story of Ramayana composed by Maharshi Valmiki”. He further declared, “if you weigh in a scale, all Vedas, Upanishads and Agama Shastras, the word ‘Rama’ the name of my Lord, proves of equal weight for the same”. He named his work as Ramayana Kalpa Vruksham. He said he composed Ramayana as Shanthi Rasa whereas Valmiki composed as Karuna Rasa.

    While bowing to ancient poets like Bhasa, Kalidasa, Bhava Bhooti, he described them as commentators of the work of Ramayana. The statement reflects that he was himself also a commentator on Ramayana. The nature of commentary on Ramayana is witnessed at every stage of his work as he understood the depth of each event spiritually. It is said about Valmiki that he knows Ramayana not intellectually but spiritually. Sri Aurobindo said,

    “His (Valmiki’s) knowledge is one, not with his thought, but with his being, he has not arrived at it, but has it in himself by virtue of his power to become one with all that is around him – he knows thus spirituality and not rationally.” This equally applies to our Poet.

    Thus, when we see his Prajna, Pratibha, faith and fervour and philosophy and his achievements and the positions occupied by him and his missionary zeal for the movement of renaissance would impel us to say that he was a Bhrasta Yogi – fallen yogi in his previous life. Bhagavan Veda Vyasa said, a fallen Yogi will be born in a prosperous family or in the family of a pious Yogi and he becomes endowed with wisdom acquired in the previous body and he strives more than before for perfection. (B.G.6-43)

    Hence, the Telugus should not forget his message to instill the faith in every one to secure Kavya Rasa by reading and disseminating the literary knowledge given by sages and poets of this country through their works.

    Foreword

    Cosmology, Cosmogony, Cosmography are the three words used with reference to the study of the Universe. Cosmology is the science of the formation of the Universe, consisting of five elements. Cosmogony is the study of the origin of the Universe, which can be described as the spiritual science. Cosmography is the description of the Universe and its constitution.

    This book deals with the third aspect of the Universe – cosmography. Our sages called the Universe as Brahmanda. We live in this Brahmanda – the golden egg. Under this subject, we have to study the seven upper worlds, the seven netherworlds, the Bhuloka consisting of seven dwipas and sub–divisions and the stellar region consisting of planets, stars, constellations, including the pole star and the great mountain known as Maha Meru.

    Sri Kota Venkatachalam, a Vedic scholar and a saint who participated in the national movement, is the author of two works Brahmanda Srusti Vijnanam, I Volume (in Telugu, now translated in the English), and Manava Srusti Vijnanam, II Volume (in English). In the I Volume, he broke new ground and struck a special note. He based his conclusion on the strength of a Vedic hymn - Taitiriya Brahmana Kanda, Brahmanda Purana, Surya Siddhanta, and Siddhanta Siromani and stated his conclusions

    • The entire Universe was in the form of water in which was found a lotus leaf supported by a long stem. Prajapati in the form of a boar dipped into the water and discovered the earth at the root of the stem. He picked up a small fragment of clay and spread it on the lotus leaf which is termed as Pruthvi
    • The earth that was placed on the leaf is the globe on which we live – Bhugola. The earth from which the wet clay was brought to make Bhugola is Mahabhumi.
    • The six dwipas and a portion of Jambu dwipa are in the Mahabumi and the Bhugola or the globe on which we live is only a portion of Jambu dwipa.
    • The Jambu dwipa which is the globe on which we live alone was divided into nine varshas, but these varshas subsequently got degraded during the course of time.
    • With reference to the measure of time of Manvantaras, we are in the seventh Manvantara known as Vaivasvata Manvantara. The Manu who is ruling the present Manvantara is in the Bharat Khanda of Jambu dwipa portion of Mahabumi, along with sages.

    The author further said that the distinction found in the original puranas between Mahabumi and Bhuloka cannot be understood by foreigners and hence some of the religious associations destroyed all those works as stated by Louise Jacoliot. (see page ).

    With reference to the seven upper worlds, we have got a Vedic hymn which says “Om Bhuhu Om Bhuhvah, OGum Suvah, Om Mahah Om Janah, Om Tapah, OGum Satyam”.

    Sri Aurobindo gave the principle governing the higher regions of the universe which is of immense value. 1. Pure Existence – Sat: Worlds of the highest truth of being (Satyaloka); 2. Pure consciousness – Chit: World of infinite Will or conscious force (Tapoloka); 3. Pure Bliss – Ananda: World of creative delight of existence (Janaloka); 4. Knowledge or Truth – Vijnana: World of the Vastness (Maharloka); 5. Mind: World of light (Swar); 6. Life (nervous being): World of various becoming (Buvar); 7. Matter: The material world (Bhur). (Page 23 Volume-11 Birth Centenary Library)

    He further stated that the three highest worlds are classed together as the triple Divine Principle, for they dwell always together in a Trinity. Infinity is their scope; bliss is their foundation.

    Similarly, Bhagavata Mahapurana (SK2, Chapter 1) mentions seven Netherlands. They are, Atala, Vitala, Sutala, RAsatala, Talatala, Mahatala and Patala. For the meditation of the cosmic form, Patala is the underside of his feet. The heels and the upper part of the feet constitute the Rasatala, and the Satyaloka is the head of the multi-headed being covering all the 14 worlds. Similarly, it is said the stellar sphere is represented in the form of Sisumara or in the shape of dolphin. Our sages used to declare that the stellar sphere is capable of being meditated upon through deep concentration of mind as a form of Lord. At the end of the tail of this divine Sisumara, or dolphin, the pole star exists. At its tail, below the end, other stars representing the Prajapatis like Kasyapa, Agni, Indra, Dharma stay. This is the form of meditation on the luminous constellations.

    It is true that some of the utterances cannot be verified by senses. Undoubtedly sages discovered these things at the level of soul. It is clear that even in respect of five elements of the universe, initially scientists only took into account water, fire, air. It is Aristotle that included earth as an element. Even today, the scientists are not visualizing that space is an element. From the inception we have got the Vedic hymn declaring “Prthivyapasthejo Vayurakas” – Earth, Water, Fire, Air, Space and “Sabdha Sparsa Rupa Rasa Gandhah” – the essence of five elements of space, air, fire, water and earth are: Sound, Touch, Form, Taste, Scent respectively.

    Similarly cosmogony, the origin of the universe, consists of three unmanifest powers (Manasa, Mahat and Ahamkara). Ahamkara is the common consciousness, Mahat is the common intellect, and Manasa is the cosmic energy with all its tendencies – Satva, Rajas and Tamas. Though these concepts cannot be physically verified, their play in the universe can be clearly understood – A cow is gentle, horse is active and the tiger is cruel. Similarly, one man gentle, another emotional and a third cruel. Further, Sage Vedavyasa said the human body is a miniature of the universe consisting of five organs of sense, five organs of actions, five vital airs, and four internal organs.

    However, we must note that the modern scientists are prepared to rely upon epistemology as a tool of understanding apart from the traditional procedure of empiricism.

    In his work of Mahabharata, Sage Vedavyasa gave three lessons on the concept of time; the play of time, the measure of time, and the quality of time. It is astounding to note in the measure of time, apart from yugas and Kalpas, ‘1’ followed by sixty two zeros have special names, namely Ogha and Mahaogha. Similarly, in the history of the universe, he gave several truths not known to humanity. He said the first creation is through Prakrti, five elements. The second creation is through the couple under procreation. He also said that in the ancient times, people got offspring by will, word of mouth, by sight and by touch and sexual union at a later period (Asramavasa Parva, Chapter 30). He also mentions instances of instantaneous birth described as Sadyogarbham, the conception immediately followed by birth.

    He also said more than once that we are in the seventh Manvantara of the “Sweta VAraha Kalpa” creation. He mentioned the names of the Earth as Prthvi, Medini, Kasyapi, Urvi and how it acquired these names with reference to ancient history several Kalpas back.

    Professor R.L. Thompson, in his Mysteries of the Sacred Universe, appreciated the true account of cosmology given in Bhagavata Purana and observed that we have not specifically studied the Vedic texts from cosmological stand point. Hence, it is beneficial for us to read this work of Venkatachalam who took the Vedic hymn along with the commentary of Vidyaranya for his conclusion on the cosmography. Geography includes physical features, population, climate and other particulars. Hence he gave these particulars in different parts of the universe; including the respective rulers of their areas with reference to the measures of time known as Manvantaras and Kalpas.

    In the second book – Manava Srishti Vignanam, the author stated that time since creation began upto 1944 A.D. is 195,58,85,044 years with authoritative data. He establishes by evidence that the knowledge of Aryans is the base of Veda. They lived at the banks of river Saraswathi known as Brahmavartadesa. Hence, he drew a map showing clearly Brahmarshi Desa, Madhya Desa, Aryavartha Desa, Yajna Desa and Bharata Varsha. This book clearly demonstrate that the history of this country must be re-written for restoring its glory. Thus, he collected historical data which was done in a marvelous way, as he is known as an eminent historian.

    I must say that the author is not a mere scholar. He is a person having Atma Jnana (Self Knowledge) and he is a philosopher. He had practically lead life of a saint and lived on fruits for 20 years, traveled entire country by foot. His work “Advaita Bhodhini and Nirvicara Jeevanam” clearly establish that he had practiced what he had learnt from sages and ultimately took Sanyasasramam. Hence, his utterances are of great value to humanity.

    Finally, I must say he struck a very beneficial note in the first book Brahmanda Sristi Vijnanam stating, that two Itihasas – Srimad Ramayana and Maha Bharata occurred in the very Vaiswata Manvantaram in which we are living occurring in successive Maha Yugas and they describe the dynasties of Kings from the beginning of creation.

    They give not only the names of Kings and their descendents, but also the nature of human beings at that time and their habits, virtues and culture, education and government and spiritual traits, aspirations in the worldly and spiritual goals in great detail. He also observed they were written in such a manner that in spite of many disasters, many revolutions, many destructions by vandals and many natural calamities, they could be memorized and preserved by the sages for posterity. Hence, they are available to us even now.

    The knowledge given by him has become a forgotten knowledge. It is imperative that the democracy must be spiritualized and every citizen must be prepared to sacrifice for humanity, which is the message of the Vedic hymns embodied in these two epics - Ramayana and Mahabharata.

    Sri G.S. Murthy a scientist and a scholar translated the work precisely with all earnestness, and Sri Avadhani, founder of Veda Bharati, took a keen interest in preparing the script of these two books and edited them.
